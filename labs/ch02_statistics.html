Key Takeaways
  • Normal distribution is fully specified by μ (mean) and σ (standard deviation). The empirical 68-95-99.7 rule describes how data clusters.
  • Poisson distribution models counts of rare events in a fixed interval. It approaches Normal as λ → ∞.
  • Exponential distribution models waiting times between Poisson events and has the memoryless property: P(X>s+t|X>s) = P(X>t).
  • The Beta distribution is conjugate to the Binomial — it's the natural prior for probabilities, bounded in [0,1].

Theorem Statement
As n → ∞:
x̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n)
With n=30, x̄ is approximately Normal regardless of the source distribution.
Key Takeaways
  • The CLT explains why so many natural phenomena are approximately Normal — they are sums or averages of many independent processes.
  • n ≥ 30 is the traditional rule of thumb for CLT to kick in. Highly skewed distributions require larger n.
  • The standard error σ/√n shrinks with sample size — this is why larger studies give more precise estimates.
  • The CLT underpins confidence intervals, t-tests, and regression inference — it is arguably the most important theorem in applied statistics.

Key Takeaways
  • The p-value is the probability of observing a result at least as extreme as the data, assuming H₀ is true. It is NOT the probability that H₀ is true.
  • Significance level α is the threshold we set before the experiment. Typical values: 0.05, 0.01, 0.001.
  • Type I error (false positive) = α. Type II error (false negative) = β. These are inversely related — reducing α increases β.
  • Statistical power increases with larger n and larger effect size. A study may be statistically non-significant simply due to being underpowered.

Bayes' Formula
P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H) / P(E)
P(D|+) = sens·prev / [sens·prev + (1−spec)·(1−prev)]
Base rate neglect: When a disease is rare, even a highly accurate test produces mostly false positives. The posterior is dominated by P(D).
Key Takeaways
  • Prior × Likelihood → Posterior. Bayes' theorem is the mathematically correct way to update beliefs when new evidence arrives.
  • Base rate neglect is one of the most common errors in probabilistic reasoning. A 95% accurate test on a 1% prevalence disease has ~9% true positive rate after a positive test.
  • In Bayesian ML, the prior encodes our beliefs before seeing data; the likelihood is what the data tells us; the posterior combines both.
  • Naive Bayes classifiers, Bayesian neural networks, and probabilistic graphical models all use this framework as their foundation.
